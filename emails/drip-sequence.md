If 10 seconds feels easy, good — we're building tendon tolerance first. Tendons adapt 3-6x slower than muscle (Magnusson et al., J Physiol, 2010). Rushing this is how you get medial epicondylitis.

## Email 2 — Day 7

**Subject:** Week 2: Add 5 seconds
**Preview:** Your tendons are adapting. Time to load them.

Week 1 done. Here's Week 2:

**3 rounds x 15 seconds | 60 seconds rest**

You should feel the difference in your forearm extensors already. Grip endurance typically improves 12-18% in the first two weeks of consistent hanging (Bohannon, Clin Interv Aging, 2019).

### This week's focus: Scapular awareness
During your hang, notice whether your shoulders are by your ears (passive) or slightly pulled down (active). Both are useful:

- **Passive hang** — decompresses the spine, stretches lats and pecs
- **Active hang** — engages lower traps and serratus anterior, builds shoulder stability

Alternate between them. 2 rounds passive, 1 round active.

**Preview:** The Lancet study that changed how we think about ageing.

Week 3 protocol:

**3 rounds x 20 seconds | 60 seconds rest**

You're now hanging for a full minute per session. Here's why that matters:

In 2015, the PURE study tracked 139,691 adults across 17 countries. They found each 5 kg decrease in grip strength was associated with a 16% increase in all-cause mortality and a 17% increase in cardiovascular mortality (Leong et al., Lancet, 2015).

Grip strength outperformed systolic blood pressure as a predictor of death.

You're not just training your forearms. You're training a biomarker.

If your hang time hasn't improved much this week, that's expected. Neural adaptations (better motor unit recruitment) dominate weeks 1-3. Structural adaptations — tendon collagen remodelling, muscle fibre hypertrophy — take longer.

### Quick self-test
Hang from the bar with just your fingertips (open grip, no thumb wrap). If you can hold 5+ seconds, your finger flexors are adapting well. If not, keep training — you're building the foundation.

Your rest periods increased to 75 seconds this week. Use them fully.

## Email 6 — Day 35

**Subject:** Week 6: Spinal decompression — what the research says
**Preview:** Hanging isn't just for grip. Your spine benefits too.

Week 6 protocol:

**4 rounds x 25 seconds | 60 seconds rest**

Orthopaedic surgeon John Kirsch has documented improvements in shoulder impingement symptoms through regular overhead hanging protocols. His hypothesis: hanging restores the subacromial space by reshaping the acromion and coracoacromial ligament over time.

### The decompression angle
Axial loading from sitting compresses intervertebral discs throughout the day. Hanging creates traction in the opposite direction. While research on disc rehydration from hanging specifically is limited, spinal traction has established evidence for reducing intradiscal pressure (Pellecchia, J Orthop Sports Phys Ther, 1994).

Hang after your workday. Your spine will thank you.

## Email 8 — Day 49

**Subject:** Week 8: Variations to break through
**Preview:** Same bar. Different grips. New stimulus.

Week 8 protocol:

**4 rounds x 30 seconds | 60 seconds rest**
Round 1-2: Overhand grip (pronated)
Round 3: Underhand grip (supinated)
Round 4: Mixed grip (one hand over, one under)

Grip variation changes which forearm muscles are primarily loaded:
- **Overhand** — flexor digitorum profundus + superficialis
- **Underhand** — adds biceps brachii and brachialis as stabilisers
- **Mixed** — trains anti-rotation through the torso

Variety prevents overuse patterns and builds more complete forearm development.

## Email 9 — Day 56

**Subject:** Week 9: Why grip strength declines with age
**Preview:** Sarcopenia starts at 30. Hanging fights it.

Week 9 protocol:

**4 rounds x 35 seconds | 75 seconds rest**

Skeletal muscle mass declines approximately 3-8% per decade after age 30, accelerating after 60 (Volpi et al., Curr Opin Clin Nutr Metab Care, 2004). Grip strength follows the same curve.

This is sarcopenia — the age-related loss of muscle mass and function. It's the reason falls become dangerous. It's the reason getting out of a chair becomes hard.

Grip strength is one of the earliest measurable markers of sarcopenia. And unlike a DEXA scan, you can test it with a $30 dynamometer or a pull-up bar.

You are currently reversing your personal curve. Keep hanging.
